At a Glance: GLP-1 Weight-Loss Medications

What they are

GLP-1 medications mimic a hormone that helps regulate appetite and blood sugar. Some versions are approved specifically for chronic weight management when combined with diet and exercise.

Typical weight-loss range

  • In clinical trials, many people lost around 10–15% of their body weight or more over 1–1.5 years when combined with lifestyle changes.
  • Results vary widely by individual, dose, duration, lifestyle, and the specific medication.

Common side effects

  • Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea or constipation, abdominal discomfort, and decreased appetite.
  • Rare but serious risks can include pancreatitis, gallbladder issues, and potential thyroid-related concerns.
These medications are powerful tools but not magic. They work best as part of a long-term plan that includes nutrition, movement, and sleep—and under close medical supervision.

How GLP-1 Weight-Loss Medications Work

GLP-1 (glucagon-like peptide-1) is a hormone your body releases after you eat. It helps control blood sugar and sends “fullness” signals to your brain. GLP-1 medications are prescription drugs that mimic or enhance this hormone. Some versions are approved for type 2 diabetes, and specific formulations are also approved for chronic weight management in people who meet certain criteria.

Why they can be so effective

  • Reduced appetite: Many people feel fuller faster and less interested in snacking between meals.
  • Slower stomach emptying: Food moves more slowly from the stomach to the intestines, which can help stabilize blood sugar and prolong satiety.
  • Improved metabolic health: Some people see improvements in blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol when used as part of a comprehensive plan.

Clinical trials have shown that GLP-1-based medications, when combined with diet and exercise, can help many people lose a meaningful percentage of their body weight over time. However, not everyone responds the same way, and some regain weight if they stop treatment without a solid lifestyle plan in place.

Who GLP-1 Programs Are (and Aren’t) For

Ultimately, only a licensed clinician can decide whether a GLP-1 medication is appropriate for you. In general, weight-loss-focused GLP-1 programs are typically designed for:

People who may be good candidates

  • Adults with obesity (often BMI ≥ 30) who have not achieved sufficient results with lifestyle changes alone.
  • Adults with overweight (often BMI ≥ 27) plus at least one weight-related condition, such as high blood pressure, high cholesterol, or sleep apnea.
  • People who are prepared to make ongoing lifestyle changes and attend regular follow-ups.

People who often should not use GLP-1s

  • Anyone with a personal or family history of certain thyroid cancers or multiple endocrine neoplasia type 2 (MEN2).
  • People who are pregnant, trying to conceive, or breastfeeding.
  • People with a history of serious allergic reactions to GLP-1 medications or their ingredients.
  • People with certain kidney, gastrointestinal, or gallbladder conditions, depending on the medication and severity.
This list is not exhaustive. Your clinician will review your full medical history, current medications, and lab results to decide whether a GLP-1 is an appropriate option and, if so, which specific drug and dose to use.

Risks, Side Effects, and Safety Tips

Like all prescription medications, GLP-1s come with potential side effects and risks. Many people experience at least some mild digestive issues when starting or adjusting the dose. Serious complications are less common but possible.

Common side effects

  • Nausea, vomiting, or queasiness, especially when first starting or after dose increases.
  • Diarrhea, constipation, bloating, or abdominal pain.
  • Decreased appetite or feeling full sooner than usual.
  • Headache, fatigue, or mild dizziness in some people.

More serious risks to discuss with your clinician

  • Pancreatitis (inflammation of the pancreas).
  • Gallbladder problems, including gallstones.
  • Potential changes in kidney function, particularly if you get dehydrated from vomiting or diarrhea.
  • Possible thyroid-related concerns seen in animal studies; human risk is still being studied.

Basic safety tips

  • Follow the dosing schedule exactly and do not increase your dose faster than recommended.
  • Contact your care team if side effects are severe, persistent, or feel “not normal” for you.
  • Stay hydrated and aim for regular meals with protein, fiber, and healthy fats to support your GI system.
  • Never use GLP-1 medications obtained without a prescription or from unverified sources.
If you have severe abdominal pain, trouble breathing, yellowing of the skin or eyes, or other urgent symptoms, seek emergency care or call your local emergency number right away.
